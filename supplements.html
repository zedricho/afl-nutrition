Supplements & Sports Foods

Evidence-based guidance on supplements and sports nutrition products

Understanding Supplements

Supplements can play a role in an athlete's nutrition plan, but they should never replace a well-planned diet. Before considering any supplement, ensure your foundation nutrition is solid.

Anti-Doping Considerations

All AFL players are subject to ASADA testing. Some supplements may contain banned substances or contaminants. Always check products are batch-tested (Informed Sport or HASTA certification) and consult with your club's medical staff before using any supplement (Maughan et al., 2018).

Evidence-Based Supplements

Caffeine

  • What it does: Improves alertness, concentration, and reduces perceived effort during exercise
  • Effective dose: 3–6mg/kg body weight, taken 30-60 minutes before exercise
  • Sources: Coffee, caffeine tablets, energy gels, pre-workout supplements
  • Considerations: Can disrupt sleep if taken too late; individual responses vary

Creatine

  • What it does: Increases phosphocreatine stores, supporting high-intensity repeated efforts (sprints, contests)
  • Effective dose: 3–5g/day consistently. Loading phase (20g/day for 5-7 days) optional
  • Considerations: May cause slight weight gain (water retention). Safe for long-term use (Kreider et al., 2017)

Protein Powder

  • What it does: Convenient source of high-quality protein to support muscle repair
  • When useful: Post-training, travel, or to help meet daily protein targets
  • Types: Whey (fast-absorbing), Casein (slow-release for before bed), Plant-based options

Antioxidants

  • What it does: Minimise damage to skeletal muscle, reducing muscle fatigue, inflammation and soreness (König et al., 2001)
  • Effective dose: 500–1000 mg/day of Vitamin C; 400–500 mg twice daily of specific antioxidants for recovery
  • Sources: Vitamin C, Vitamin E, blueberries, leafy greens, dark chocolate, nuts and seeds, turmeric
  • Considerations: Potential for diarrhea, nausea, and abdominal cramps with Vitamin C intakes >2000 mg/day

Beetroot Juice

  • What it does: Dilates blood vessels in exercising muscle, reduces oxygen use and improves energy production (Lidder & Webb, 2013)
  • Effective dose: Approximately 2 cups
  • Sources: Beetroots, concentrated beetroot shots (e.g., Beet It)
  • Considerations: No safety concerns reported for short-term use at commonly recommended amounts

Beta-alanine

  • What it does: Increases synthesis of carnosine which buffers changes in muscle pH, reducing muscle fatigue and loss of force production (Hobson et al., 2012)
  • Effective dose: 1.6–6.4 g/day
  • Sources: Powder form
  • Considerations: May cause tingling or itchy skin (paraesthesia) in face, neck, back of hands, and upper trunk with doses >800 mg or >10 mg/kg body mass

Sports Foods

Sports foods provide convenient nutrition for training or competition situations:

  • Energy Gels: Concentrated carbohydrates (20-30g per gel) – use during games or at half-time
  • Sports Drinks: Fluid + carbohydrates + electrolytes – during and after exercise
  • Protein Bars: Higher protein, moderate carbs – recovery snack or travel
  • Electrolyte Tablets: Sodium and electrolytes – hot conditions, heavy sweaters

Always trial sports foods during training before using them on game day.

Reducing Your Risk

  • Only use products with third-party batch testing (Informed Sport, HASTA)
  • Consult your club dietitian or doctor before starting any supplement
  • Be cautious with: fat burners, testosterone boosters, "proprietary blends," and overseas products with aggressive marketing claims
  • Check the ASADA website for current prohibited substances
