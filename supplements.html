Supplements & Sports Foods

Evidence-based guidance on supplements and sports nutrition products

Understanding Supplements

Supplements can play a role in an athlete's nutrition plan, but they should never replace a well-planned diet. Before considering any supplement, ensure your foundation nutrition is solid.

Important: Anti-Doping Considerations

All AFL players are subject to ASADA (Australian Sports Anti-Doping Authority) testing. Some supplements may contain banned substances or contaminants. Always check products are batch-tested (look for Informed Sport or HASTA certification) and consult with your club's medical staff before using any supplement.

📍 Before Taking Any Supplement, Ask:

  • Is it safe? (Check for third-party testing)
  • Is it legal? (Check ASADA and WADA prohibited lists)
  • Is it effective? (What does the research say?)
  • Is it necessary? (Could I get this from food instead?)

Evidence-Based Supplements

The following supplements have strong scientific evidence supporting their use in appropriate situations.

Caffeine

What it does: Improves alertness, concentration, and can reduce perceived effort during exercise.

Effective dose: 3-6mg per kg body weight, taken 30-60 minutes before exercise.

Sources: Coffee, caffeine tablets, energy gels with caffeine, pre-workout supplements.

Considerations: Can disrupt sleep if taken too late. Start with lower doses to assess tolerance. Individual responses vary significantly.

Creatine

What it does: Increases phosphocreatine stores, supporting high-intensity, repeated efforts (sprints, contests). May also support recovery and muscle mass.

Effective dose: 3-5g per day consistently. Loading phase (20g/day for 5-7 days) is optional.

Considerations: May cause slight weight gain (water retention). Safe for long-term use. One of the most researched and effective supplements.

Protein Powder

What it does: Convenient source of high-quality protein to support muscle repair and growth.

When useful: When whole food options aren't practical (post-training, travel), or to help meet daily protein targets.

Types: Whey (fast-absorbing, good post-exercise), Casein (slow-release, good before bed), Plant-based options available.

Considerations: Food-first approach is preferred when possible. Protein powder is a supplement, not a replacement for meals.

Sports Foods

Sports foods are specifically designed products that provide convenient nutrition for athletes in training or competition situations.

Product What It Provides When to Use
Energy Gels Concentrated carbohydrates (20-30g per gel) During games or long training; quick energy boost at half-time
Sports Bars Carbs + protein + sometimes fat Pre-training snack, travel, recovery if no other options
Sports Drinks Fluid + carbohydrates + electrolytes During and after exercise for fuel and hydration
Protein Bars Higher protein, moderate carbs Recovery snack, travel, meeting protein targets
Electrolyte Tablets Sodium and other electrolytes, minimal carbs Hot conditions, heavy sweaters, when carbs not needed

💡 Practical Tip

Always trial sports foods during training before using them on game day. Gastrointestinal issues from unfamiliar products can seriously impact performance.

What AFL Players Are Using

Based on our survey of AFL players, here's what's commonly used:

Most Common

  • Protein powder
  • Sports drinks
  • Caffeine (coffee/tablets)
  • Multivitamins

Also Used

  • Creatine
  • Fish oil / Omega-3
  • Vitamin D
  • Energy gels
"I mainly stick to protein powder and creatine - they're the ones that actually have evidence behind them. I've tried other stuff but didn't notice any difference." - AFL Player Survey Respondent

Supplements to Be Cautious About

Many supplements on the market have little evidence supporting their use, or carry risks of contamination.

Higher Risk Products

Be particularly cautious with: fat burners, testosterone boosters, "proprietary blends" with undisclosed ingredients, products from overseas with aggressive marketing claims, and any product promising rapid results.

Reduce Your Risk

  • Only use products with third-party batch testing (Informed Sport, HASTA)
  • Consult your club dietitian or doctor before starting any supplement
  • Be skeptical of dramatic claims - if it sounds too good to be true, it probably is
  • Check the ASADA website for current prohibited substances
