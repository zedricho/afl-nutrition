Recovery Nutrition Strategies

Optimise recovery to perform at your best, day after day

Why Recovery Nutrition Matters

Recovery nutrition is one of the most important yet often overlooked aspects of an AFL player's diet. With multiple training sessions per week and games every 6-7 days, your body needs to recover quickly and efficiently.

📍 The 3 R's of Recovery

  • Refuel: Replenish glycogen (carbohydrate) stores in muscles
  • Repair: Provide protein for muscle repair and adaptation
  • Rehydrate: Replace fluids and electrolytes lost through sweat

Glycogen Replenishment

Glycogen is your muscles' primary fuel source during high-intensity AFL activities. After training or games, these stores are depleted and need to be refilled.

How Much Carbohydrate?

  • Immediately post-exercise: 1-1.2g carbohydrate per kg body weight
  • For an 85kg player: 85-100g carbohydrates in the first hour
  • Continue eating: Regular carbohydrate-rich meals over the next 24 hours
Food Portion Carbohydrates
Banana 1 large ~30g
White rice (cooked) 1 cup ~45g
Sports drink (600ml) 1 bottle ~36g
Pasta (cooked) 1 cup ~40g
Chocolate milk 500ml ~50g
Jam sandwich (white bread) 2 slices ~45g

Protein for Muscle Repair

Training and games cause muscle damage that needs to be repaired. Protein provides the building blocks (amino acids) for this repair process.

Post-Exercise Protein

Aim for 20-40g of high-quality protein within 1-2 hours after exercise to maximise muscle protein synthesis.

Daily Protein Needs

AFL players should aim for 1.6-2.2g protein per kg body weight, spread across the day.

High-Quality Protein Sources (20-30g protein)

  • Chicken breast (100-120g)
  • Greek yoghurt (200g)
  • Eggs (3-4 whole eggs)
  • Lean beef or fish (100g)
  • Protein shake (1 scoop / 25-30g powder)
  • Cottage cheese (200g)

💡 Protein Distribution

Spreading protein intake evenly across meals (4-5 serves of 20-40g) is more effective for muscle recovery than consuming most protein in one meal.

"Fluid and protein intake matters a lot." - AFL Half Back Flank, 5 years at AFL level (advice to his younger self)

Sleep and Recovery Nutrition

Sleep is when much of your recovery happens. Nutrition can support better sleep quality and overnight recovery.

Pre-Sleep Nutrition

  • Protein before bed: Casein protein (found in dairy) is slowly digested and can support overnight muscle repair
  • Good options: Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese, or a casein-based shake
  • Avoid: Large, heavy meals close to bedtime that may disrupt sleep
  • Limit caffeine: Avoid caffeine within 4-6 hours of sleep

Foods & Supplements That May Support Sleep

  • Tart cherry juice (contains natural melatonin)
  • Dairy products (contain tryptophan)
  • Complex carbohydrates (may improve sleep quality)
  • Kiwifruit (linked to improved sleep in some studies)
  • Magnesium supplementation (some players report improved sleep)
"Magnesium helps my sleep." - AFL Midfielder, 6 months at AFL level

💡 Player Insight

When asked what nutrition habit has made the biggest difference to performance or recovery, one AFL midfielder specifically cited magnesium supplementation for improving sleep quality.
