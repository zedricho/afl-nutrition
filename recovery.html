Recovery Nutrition Strategies

Optimise recovery to perform at your best, day after day

Why Recovery Nutrition Matters

With multiple training sessions per week and games every 6-7 days, your body needs to recover quickly and efficiently. Recovery focuses on the 3 R's: Refuel (replenish glycogen stores), Repair (provide protein for muscle repair), and Rehydrate (replace fluids and electrolytes).

Recovery Takes Time

Research shows that a 3–4-day recovery period is often not enough to restore the body back to its normal state, leading to performance deterioration and increased likelihood of musculoskeletal injuries. Performance deterioration peaks between 24 and 48 hours post-exercise, indicating that recovery requires ongoing fueling across the following days – not just the immediate post-training window (Poulios et al., 2019).

Protein for Muscle Repair

Consuming protein post-training and games increases the rate of protein synthesis to help with muscle repair and remodelling, therefore improving performance recovery. It is vital for athletes to consume enough protein to mitigate muscle damage, inflammatory and oxidative stress markers, and muscle deterioration (Poulios et al., 2019).

The 30-Minute Window

The 30 minutes after training is the key window to start the recovery process and accumulate stores for the next session. In this time, consume:

  • 15–20g of protein
  • 1g per kg body weight of carbohydrates (e.g., 85g for an 85kg player)

(Sports Dietitians Australia, 2025)

Daily Protein Needs

AFL players should aim for 1.6–2.2g protein per kg body weight spread across the day (Kerksick et al., 2017). Spreading protein intake evenly across 4-5 meals (20-40g each) is more effective than consuming most protein in one meal.

High-Quality Protein Sources (20-30g protein)

  • Chicken breast (100-120g)
  • Greek yoghurt (200g)
  • Eggs (3-4 whole eggs)
  • Lean beef or fish (100g)
  • Protein shake (1 scoop / 25-30g powder)
"Fluid and protein intake matters a lot." - AFL Half Back Flank, 5 years at AFL level

Glycogen Replenishment

Glycogen is your muscles' primary fuel source during high-intensity AFL activities. After training or games, these stores are depleted and need to be refilled with carbohydrates.

Food Portion Carbs
White rice (cooked) 1 cup ~45g
Chocolate milk 500ml ~50g
Pasta (cooked) 1 cup ~40g
Banana 1 large ~30g
Sports drink (600ml) 1 bottle ~36g

Recovery Meal Ideas

  • Burrito bowl
  • Poke bowl
  • Healthy burger
  • Shepherd's pie

Sleep and Recovery Nutrition

Sleep is considered one of the most important recovery strategies for athletes. AFL athletes may experience poor sleep due to late games, evening training, travel demands, and elevated stress after competition – which can negatively affect recovery, immune function, and injury risk (Gratwicke et al., 2021).

Nutrition Strategies to Support Sleep

  • High GI carbohydrates: Consuming a high glycaemic index meal 2-4 hours before bed may help improve sleep onset
  • Protein before bed: 20-40g of protein supports overnight muscle recovery
  • Tart cherry juice: Can promote sleep quantity
  • Kiwifruit: Shown to improve both sleep quality and quantity
  • Limit caffeine: Avoid 4-6 hours before bed

Night Game Recovery

After late games (finishing 9-11pm), athletes face reduced appetite and limited food options. Strategies include:

  • Have portable recovery foods ready (protein shakes, bars, sandwiches)
  • If appetite is poor, liquid nutrition (smoothies, protein milk) may be easier
  • Focus on fluid and carbohydrate replacement immediately; protein can follow
