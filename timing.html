Nutrition Timing

When to eat for optimal performance and recovery

Why Timing Matters

While overall daily nutrition is most important, timing your intake around training and games can help you perform better and recover faster. This is especially relevant for AFL players with multiple training sessions per week and short recovery windows between games (Kerksick et al., 2017).

Key Timing Principles

  • Fuel appropriately before exercise to maximise performance
  • Consume carbohydrates during prolonged/intense sessions
  • Prioritise protein and carbohydrates after exercise for recovery
  • Spread protein intake throughout the day (4-5 serves of 20-40g)
  • Consider pre-sleep nutrition for overnight recovery

Before Training & Games

Time Before What to Eat Examples
3-4 hours Complete meal: carbs, moderate protein, low fat/fibre Chicken and rice, pasta with lean meat
1-2 hours Light snack, easily digestible carbs Banana, toast with honey, muesli bar
<1 hour Quick energy if needed Sports drink, few lollies, small piece of fruit

Carbohydrate Timing

Carbohydrates are your muscles' primary fuel source. Strategic timing ensures you have energy when you need it most.

  • High training load days: 5-8g/kg body weight, with carbs at each meal and prioritised around training
  • Low training load / rest days: 3-5g/kg body weight, focus on protein, vegetables, and healthy fats
  • Game day: Carb-rich meals 24-48 hours before; pre-game meal 3-4 hours before focused on carbs

Evening Nutrition and Sleep

Research shows that evening nutrition is more strongly associated with sleep outcomes than total daily intake (Falkenberg et al., 2021). This is particularly relevant for AFL players training or playing late.

Evening Food Choices and Sleep

  • Higher evening sugar intake was associated with shorter total sleep time
  • Evening protein intake was associated with reduced sleep onset latency (falling asleep faster)

While these relationships are relatively small, they suggest that food choices in the evening period may influence how well athletes sleep after training.

Optimising Evening Intake

There is no strict cut-off time for eating, but optimising evening intake may involve:

  • Limiting high-sugar foods close to bedtime
  • Including protein-based foods as part of recovery nutrition
  • Ensuring overall recovery intake is still sufficient after training

Night Games

After late finishes, balance recovery nutrition with sleep. Prioritise protein-based recovery foods over high-sugar options, but ensure you still consume adequate carbohydrates and fluid for recovery. See the Recovery page for more strategies.
